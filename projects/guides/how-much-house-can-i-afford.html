How much house can I afford?

Before you fall in love with a listing, it pays to know your real number. The honest answer isn't a single figure — it's whatever keeps the monthly payment comfortable after taxes, insurance and your other debts. The 28/36 rule — the starting point

Most lenders sanity-check affordability with two ratios. Stay under both and a home is very likely within reach:

  • 28% — the housing ratio. Your total housing cost (mortgage principal + interest + property tax + insurance, sometimes called PITI) should be at most 28% of your gross (pre-tax) monthly income.
  • 36% — the total-debt ratio. Housing plus every other monthly debt — car loans, student loans, minimum credit-card payments — should be at most 36% of gross income. This is your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio.

Example: on a $6,000/month gross income, aim for roughly $1,680 or less on housing (28%) and $2,160 or less on all debt combined (36%). Some loan programs stretch to ~43% DTI, but the 28/36 line is where the payment stays comfortable, not just approvable.

Work backwards from a payment you can live with

Rather than starting from a house price, start from a monthly payment you're happy to make, then find the price that produces it. Four levers move that payment:

  • Down payment. A larger deposit lowers the amount borrowed and, at 20% or more, usually removes mortgage insurance (PMI). Even 5–10% changes the picture a lot.
  • Interest rate. A single percentage point can swing the payment by hundreds a month. It's worth modelling a rate slightly above today's quote as a buffer.
  • Loan term. A 30-year loan has a lower monthly payment but far more total interest than a 15-year; a shorter term costs more monthly but builds equity faster.
  • Taxes & insurance. These vary hugely by location and are easy to underestimate — they can add 20–30% on top of the principal-and-interest figure.

The fastest way to test combinations is to change one number at a time in the mortgage calculator and watch the monthly payment and total interest update. Try an extra-payment amount too — it shows how much interest a little extra each month saves over the life of the loan.

The costs people forget

The mortgage payment is only part of the picture. Budget for the rest so you're not house-rich and cash-poor:

  • Property taxes — billed annually or rolled into your payment via escrow.
  • Homeowners insurance — required by lenders; varies by area and rebuild cost.
  • Mortgage insurance (PMI) — typically required when the down payment is under 20%; it falls away as you build equity.
  • HOA / maintenance fees — condos and managed communities charge monthly dues.
  • Repairs & upkeep — a common rule is to set aside ~1% of the home's value per year for maintenance.
  • Closing costs — usually 2–5% of the price, paid up front on top of the deposit.

A quick affordability checklist

  • ☐ Housing cost ≤ 28% of gross monthly income
  • ☐ All debt (housing + loans + cards) ≤ 36% of gross income
  • ☐ Down payment saved, ideally 20% to skip PMI
  • ☐ Separate fund for closing costs (2–5% of price)
  • ☐ Emergency fund still intact after the down payment
  • ☐ Monthly budget tested against a rate 1% above today's quote
