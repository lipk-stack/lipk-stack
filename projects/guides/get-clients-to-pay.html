How to get clients to pay invoices on time

Late payment is the single biggest cash-flow killer for freelancers and small studios. The good news: most of it is preventable with a few habits set before the work starts. Here's the system — plus exactly what to send when an invoice goes overdue. Need to send one now? Use the free invoice & quote generator (runs in your browser, nothing uploaded, exports to PDF).

1. Agree the money before you start

Most late payments trace back to terms that were never made explicit. Lock these down in writing — even a short email counts — before the first hour of work:

  • Price and scope: what's included, and what counts as extra.
  • A deposit: 25–50% up front for new clients. It filters out non-serious buyers and means you're never fully exposed.
  • Payment terms: "due on receipt" or "Net 7" beats the default "Net 30" — shorter terms get paid sooner without complaint if you set them early.
  • A late fee: e.g. 1.5% per month overdue. You won't always charge it, but naming it up front makes paying on time the path of least resistance.

2. Send an invoice that's easy to pay

Friction delays payment. A clean, unambiguous invoice removes every excuse to wait:

  • Invoice the same day the work ships — momentum matters, and you're freshest in the client's mind.
  • State a specific due date ("Due 27 June 2026"), not just "Net 14" — a real date is harder to ignore.
  • Put the total at the top, itemise underneath, and include your payment details and an invoice number.
  • Offer more than one way to pay (bank transfer, card, PayPal/Wise) — whatever is one click for the client.

3. The polite follow-up sequence for late invoices

When a payment slips past its due date, a calm, scheduled sequence works far better than one frustrated email weeks later. Stay warm and factual — most late payments are forgetfulness, not malice:

  • Day the invoice is due — a friendly nudge: "Just a heads-up that invoice #1024 is due today — here it is again for convenience. Thanks!"
  • +3 days overdue — short reminder: restate the amount, the invoice number and the original due date. Re-attach the PDF.
  • +7 days — firmer, still friendly: ask if there's a problem on their end and confirm the payment method. Mention the late fee if you set one.
  • +14 days — clear next step: state that work pauses until the balance clears, and propose a specific payment date.

4. When a client still won't pay

  • Pause work — never keep delivering to a non-payer.
  • Get on a call. A two-minute conversation resolves more than ten emails; people avoid paying email, not faces.
  • Offer a payment plan if cash flow is the real issue — partial payment beats none.
  • Send a final notice with a firm date before escalating to a formal demand letter or small-claims process.
  • Withhold deliverables/source files until paid, if your contract allows it.

Quick checklist

  • ☐ Terms, deposit and due date agreed in writing before starting
  • ☐ Invoice sent the same day work ships, with a specific due date
  • ☐ Multiple, low-friction payment options offered
  • ☐ A scheduled, polite follow-up sequence ready for overdue invoices
  • ☐ A clear escalation path for genuine non-payers
