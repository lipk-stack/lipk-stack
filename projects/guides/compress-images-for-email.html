How to compress images for email without losing quality

You attach a few photos from your phone, hit send, and the message bounces: “attachment too large.” Modern cameras produce 4–12 MB files, and just two or three blow past an email's size limit. The fix isn't to crop or screenshot — it's to compress, which shrinks the file while keeping the picture looking the same. Here's why it happens, the settings that matter, and a free way to do it privately. Want to skip ahead? Why your photos won't send

Email providers cap how much a single message can carry. The common limits:

  • Gmail: ~25 MB per email (all attachments combined).
  • Outlook.com: ~20 MB.
  • Many work/corporate servers: 10 MB or even less.

Two catches make it worse than it sounds. First, the limit covers the whole message, so three 9 MB photos already exceed Gmail's cap. Second, attachments are re-encoded for transit and grow roughly 33%, so a 20 MB folder of files can hit the wall at 25 MB. Aiming for under 10 MB total keeps you safely clear and makes the email download fast for whoever receives it.

The two levers that shrink a photo

You don't need editing skills — just two settings, and they do almost all the work:

  • Quality (compression). JPEG and WebP throw away detail the eye barely notices. Dropping from maximum to about 0.8 quality typically cuts the file size by half or more with no visible change. Going much below ~0.6 is where softness and blocky artifacts start to show.
  • Dimensions (resize). A 12-megapixel phone photo is ~4000 px wide — far more than any screen or inbox needs. Resizing the longest edge to about 1600 px (or 1024 px for quick previews) is the single biggest saving, because file size scales with the number of pixels, not just the quality.

Combine both and a 6 MB photo routinely becomes 200–400 KB — small enough to attach a dozen at once. The fastest way to find the sweet spot is to change one setting at a time in the image compressor and watch the before/after size update for each file.

Which format should you choose?

  • JPEG — the safe default for photographs. Universally supported and small; ideal when the image is a photo of people, places or products.
  • WebP — modern format that's noticeably smaller than JPEG at the same quality. Great for web use and supported by every current browser and email client; pick this when you want the absolute smallest file.
  • PNG — lossless, and the right choice for screenshots, logos and graphics with sharp text or transparency. It does not shrink with the quality slider, so for PNGs the resize lever is what reduces the size.

Rule of thumb: photo → JPEG (or WebP for the smallest file); screenshot or logo → PNG.

A quick recipe by use case

  • Emailing a few photos: JPEG, quality 0.8, max width 1600 px. Keep the whole batch under ~10 MB.
  • Posting to a website or blog: WebP, quality 0.8, max width 1600–2000 px for fast page loads and better SEO.
  • Marketplace / classified listing: JPEG, quality 0.8, max width 1200 px — plenty of detail, uploads instantly.
  • Sharing a screenshot: PNG, resize to the width you need; no quality loss.

Keep it private
