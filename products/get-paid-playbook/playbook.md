# The Get-Paid Playbook
### How freelancers and small businesses invoice clearly, get paid faster, and chase late payments without the awkwardness

*A practical, no-fluff field guide. Copy the templates, change the bracketed
bits, send. Not legal advice — adapt to your country and contracts.*

---

## Why most invoices get paid late

Late payment is rarely about a client refusing to pay. It's almost always one of
four fixable things:

1. **The invoice was unclear** — no due date, vague line items, or a confusing total.
2. **It was easy to ignore** — buried in an email thread with no clear "what to do next".
3. **There was no agreed deadline** — "net 30" was never actually stated up front.
4. **Nobody followed up** — the first reminder is what gets 80% of late invoices paid.

Fix those four and you fix most of your cash-flow problems. This playbook is the
scripts and checklists to do exactly that.

---

## Part 1 — Set payment terms *before* you start (the 2-minute conversation)

The single highest-leverage habit: state terms in writing before any work begins.
One short message removes 90% of payment disputes.

> Hi [Name] — excited to start. To keep things clean on my end, here are the terms:
> • Total: [amount / rate]
> • Deposit: [50%] due before I begin; balance due on delivery
> • Payment terms: due within [14] days of the invoice date
> • Late payments: a [1.5%] monthly fee applies after [14] days
> • I accept [bank transfer / Wise / PayPal / card]
> If that works, just reply "agreed" and I'll send the deposit invoice today.

**Why each line matters**
- **Deposit:** the strongest cash-flow tool there is. A client who has paid a
  deposit almost always pays the rest. For new clients, 50% up front is normal.
- **A real due date:** "due within 14 days" beats "net 30" because it's concrete.
- **A late fee:** you may never charge it, but stating it changes behaviour.
- **"Reply agreed":** turns a vague chat into a written record you can point to.

## Part 2 — Build an invoice that gets paid on sight

A clear invoice answers four questions instantly: *who, what, how much, by when,
and how do I pay.* Use this structure (the free invoice generator at the end of
this guide produces exactly this layout):

```
[Your name / business]                    INVOICE #0007
[Your email · phone]                       Issue date: [date]
                                           Due date:   [date]  ← always visible

Bill to: [Client name / company]

  Description                 Qty     Rate        Amount
  ------------------------------------------------------
  [Specific deliverable]       1    [  ]        [      ]
  [Specific deliverable]       2    [  ]        [      ]
                                       Subtotal  [      ]
                                       Tax [%]   [      ]
                                       --------  -------
                                       TOTAL DUE [      ]

Payment: [bank details / payment link]
Terms: Payment due by [due date]. Thank you!
```

**The five details that get invoices paid faster**
1. **A bold, unmissable due date** near the top — not just "net 14" in small print.
2. **Specific line items.** "Website work — $2,000" invites questions.
   "Homepage design + 3 revisions — $2,000" does not.
3. **An invoice number.** It signals you're organised and makes follow-up easy.
4. **One obvious way to pay,** with the details *on the invoice* (don't make them
   ask). A clickable payment link beats raw bank details for speed.
5. **A polite one-line term** restating the due date. Friendly, not threatening.

## Part 3 — The follow-up sequence that collects late invoices

This is where most freelancers leave money on the table. They feel awkward, so
they wait. Don't. A calm, scheduled sequence is professional, not pushy — and it
works. Send these on a fixed timeline and most invoices resolve themselves.

### Email 1 — The friendly nudge (the day after the due date)
> Subject: Invoice #0007 — quick reminder
>
> Hi [Name], hope you're well! Just a gentle reminder that invoice #0007 for
> [amount] was due [yesterday/date]. No worries if it's already on its way —
> if not, here are the payment details again: [link/details]. Happy to resend
> the invoice if helpful. Thanks so much!

Tone: assume good faith. ~80% of late invoices are paid after this single email.

### Email 2 — The clear restatement (7 days overdue)
> Subject: Invoice #0007 now 1 week overdue
>
> Hi [Name], following up on invoice #0007 ([amount]), which is now a week past
> its due date of [date]. Could you let me know the expected payment date so I
> can update my records? If there's any issue with the invoice, tell me and I'll
> sort it right away. Payment details: [link/details].

Tone: still warm, but now you're asking for a *specific date*. That's the ask.

### Email 3 — The firm boundary (14 days overdue)
> Subject: Action needed: invoice #0007 — [amount] overdue
>
> Hi [Name], invoice #0007 is now 14 days overdue. As noted in our agreed terms,
> a late fee of [1.5%] now applies, bringing the balance to [new amount]. Please
> arrange payment by [date]. I value our working relationship and would like to
> keep things on track — let me know if a short payment plan would help.

Tone: professional and firm. You're referencing the terms *they agreed to*.

### Email 4 — Final notice (30 days overdue)
> Subject: Final notice before next steps — invoice #0007
>
> Hi [Name], this is a final reminder that invoice #0007 ([new amount incl. fee])
> remains unpaid 30 days after the due date. Please settle by [date]. If I don't
> hear back, I'll have to pause any current work and consider [a collections
> service / small-claims process]. I'd much rather resolve this directly — please
> get in touch today.

After this, your options depend on amount and country: a collections service, a
formal letter of demand, or small-claims court for larger sums.

## Part 4 — Habits that prevent late payments entirely

- **Always take a deposit** from new clients. It's the #1 predictor of getting
  paid in full.
- **Invoice the moment the work ships** — same day. Every day you wait pushes
  payment further out.
- **Send invoices on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning.** Monday inboxes are chaos;
  Friday invoices get forgotten over the weekend.
- **Make paying frictionless.** A one-click payment link gets paid faster than
  "here are my bank details, please add reference."
- **Keep a simple numbered log** of every invoice: number, client, amount, sent
  date, due date, paid date. You'll instantly see who's overdue.
- **Fire your worst payer.** The client who's always 45 days late costs you more
  than they're worth. Raise their deposit to 100% up front or let them go.
